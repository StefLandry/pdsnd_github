Question 1¶

When looking at Chicago ONLY, what is the distribution of Customer trip durations in minutes?

In [42]:
library(ggplot2)
chi  = read.csv('chicago.csv')
In [43]:
ggplot(data = subset(chi, User.Type == "Customer"),
       aes(x = User.Type, y = Trip.Duration/60)) +
  geom_boxplot() +
  xlab("User Type") +
  ylab("Trip Duration Minutes") +
  labs(title = "Chicago Trip Duration Distribution for Customer User Type") +
  coord_cartesian(ylim = c(0,60))

Summary of question 1: Customer users in Chicago appear to rent bicycles for durations bewteen 15 and 32 minutes. The median trip duration is roughly 22 minutes. This infers these users are going relatively short distances for this length of time. Possible scenarios could be commuting from one side of town to the other.

Question 2¶

Which city has the greatest volume of rentals?

In [45]:
ny2<- cbind(ny[1:7], Location_name = "NYC")
wash2 <- cbind(wash[1:7], Location_name = "Washington")
chi2 <- cbind(chi[1:7], Location_name = "Chicago")
first_union = merge(ny2, wash2, all=TRUE)
second_union = merge(first_union, chi2, all=TRUE)
In [46]:
ggplot(aes(x = Trip.Duration), data = second_union) +
  geom_histogram(binwidth = 50) +
  ggtitle("Histogram of Trip Counts and Durations by Location") +
  scale_x_continuous(limits = c(0, 2000), breaks = seq(0, 2000, 500)) +
  facet_wrap(~Location_name) +
  labs(x = "Rental Duration", y = "Count of Rentals")

Summary of question 2: Based on the histograms provided, we see the largest volume spikes in Washington, with the max capping out over 4,500. Chicago data shows all of their rentals are under the 500 mark. While NYC does display a behavior pattern similar to Washington, the values are smaller. It may be possible the great number of rentals and durations in Washington are driven by vacation visitors and not necessarily locals, considering the amount of travelers who visit there annually.
